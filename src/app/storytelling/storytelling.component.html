In late November, the sun does not peer into the Iceland sky until after 10:30am. At solar noon it sits at a mere 5˚, its bottom edge hovering a few fingerwidths above the horizon. At 4pm it sets, leaving Icelandic residents and visitors with a mere 5.5 hours of daylight to explore its glacial volcanic landscape...

...or so we thought. When my father initially proposed our trip to Iceland, it was with the understanding that it would include short days jam-packed with outdoor excursions, and long nights that gave us a good chance of catching the aurora borealis. What we did not anticipate, however, was the extra 2-3 hours gifted by “civil twilight" - when the sun is within 6˚ of the bottom of the horizon and still providing enough light to explore the Nordic island nation.

All of this combined to produce days spent almost entirely in the light of a setting or rising sun - a golden hour that lasted the entire day.

This project is an attempt to capture our experiences and the stunning, golden landscape that we witnessed on our 3-day trip to Iceland. It exists somewhere between a glorified travel journal and Facebook photo album with long captions - two forms of media meant more for personal reflection than mass consumption.

In the words of my grandmother, "you should know that most are not very interested in other people’s travel accounts." She’s almost certainly right. But if you feel like looking at some pictures and skimming over the journalling of someone who doesn’t actually keep a journal, then I hope you enjoy this piece.

After a brisk 5.5 hour flight from Washington, I met Henry, Dad, and Lara in the Reykjavík airport baggage claim at 6:45am Icelandic time. Henry and I quickly took the necessary steps to ensure that Jasper would be able to identify us upon his arrival an hour later, constructing a sign that would make it easier for him to spot us.

We collected our rental car - a Toyota Rav-4 equipped with “gravel insurance" for all of the rugged off-roading we had planned (I joke but there were some long gravel roads with high speed limits that the insurance was probably helpful for).

As we drove ~45 mins to our AirBnb just outside of Reykjavík, the black of the night sky started to give way to a deep blue. This was the beginning of a nearly 2.5 hour lightening of the sky before the sun finally peeked over the horizon around 10:00am.

Our AirBnb was a 3-story minimalist home dug into the hillside, with 2/3 floors buried into the ground. It overlooked the Bay of Nauthólsvík with huge, North-facing (very important) floor-to-ceiling windows. Naturally, the listing promised views of the Aurora Borealis and we were, naturally, skeptical.

The house was heated when we arrived, which we originally interpreted as a friendly owner welcoming us to a cozy abode. Later, we learned that due to Iceland’s wealth of geothermal energy, heating was basically free, leading many residents to always have their heat on full-blast and to open their windows if they need to cool it down a bit. Our AirBnb’s plumbing also took advantage of this model: the cold water came straight from the glaciers, while the hot water smelled of sulfur. While brushing my teeth, I learned the hard way that the slightest increase in water temperature would introduce sulfur into the faucet stream.

After showering, we headed into town for first planned activity - a food tour of Reykjavík that my father believed would be the perfect way to keep us up and about while we fought through our jetlag.

Our tour guide was a burly chested, blond curly wax mustachioed, 20-something Icelander named Thor (one of 1700+ permitted Icelandic male first names). He met us outside the Harpa Concert Hall wearing a thick-knitted sweater made by his grandmother and skinny jeans with holes (it was less clear who those were made by). Thor told us that he was starving and that we should start the tour immediately.

Unfortunately, none of us remembered to take photos of the food during the tour, so here is a series of written highlights instead:

Our first stop was at Kopar - a small, intimate restaurant right next to the water. Seated around a wooden table, we were served plates of Plokkfiskur: basically mashed cod and potatoes. For those of us with confused internal clocks and uneasy stomachs, this was a welcome, easy start to our day of food. Unfortunately for one member of the family, this was not a theme that would continue at our next stop.

We headed up the hill to the Dubliner - a bar with crisp Icelandic beer, hearty lamb stew, and an Icelandic delicacy that Anthony Bourdain called “unspeakably nasty" and that he would never eat again - fermented shark. Once we had all gotten our drinks and tasted the other food, Thor handed the jar of deathly-white colored shark cubes to Jasper, and told him to smell and try one. Opening the jar, Jasper took a brief whiff and immediately recoiled away from it. He looked to us with a sense of hopelessness - an expression that read “I have to eat this terrible thing, don’t I?" We laughed nervously, each of us well-aware of the likelihood of sharing his fate.

It was at this moment that Jasper made a crucial error. I fully expected him to skewer a death cube with a toothpick and pass the jar so that we could follow suit and all commit to the miserable experience together. Instead, with the jar still in front of him and his family members and - more importantly - Thor looking on, he went for it and the piece of shark disappeared into his mouth.

After some slow, forced chewing, and a pained swallow, a teary-eyed Jasper emerged victorious from this culinary test. Thor looked on proudly, the rest of us looked on in fear - as terrible as his experienced looked, there was now increased peer pressure for us to follow suit. Alas, as he turned to us excited at the prospect of watching his family members share his fate, he was met with nervous denial.

Congratulations to Jasper - the only Gordon who dared to eat the Icelandic fermented shark. For someone who grew up a very picky eater, this was a tremendous accomplishment. For the rest of us, this was a moment of tremendous shame.

There were, of course, other stops along this food tour. At Kaffi Loki we ate delicious rye bread ice cream with rhubarb syrup. We all agreed that the hot dogs we ate at Bæjarins Beztu Pylsur - topped with ketchup, sweet mustard, remoulade, crisp fried onion, and raw onion - were some of the best we have ever had. However, with the amount of space taken up by the shark episode, I think it’s time for this narrative to move on and get out of Reykjavík...

Eventually our scenic route ended with us at Blue Lagoon - a large hot springs that was described by a friend as “one of the most touristy places in Iceland" but also “unique. Cool. Incredible. Fucking do it." We were glad that we followed his advice.

To actually get in the water, we ventured through the front-desk, locker room, and showers of the Blue Lagoon resort. Once passed those obstacles though, we waded into the belly button-high, hot-but-not-too-hot waters that give the resort its name. The lagoon proved to be a bit of a labyrinth, with winding passes leading to secluded pools, bars with alcoholic beverages, and even a bar with complimentary facemasks.

An avid facemasker can purchase a tube of one of the many Signature Blue Lagoon Masks at the resort’s store, which greeted us as we exited the waters like the duty-free section at a European airport or the makeup section at a Macy’s. The tubes were also advertised heavily on the seatback monitors of our Icelandair flight.

When we originally hatched this plan, we had dreams of watching the Northern lights dance while soaking in the waters of the hot springs. Alas, the skies were cloudy and the jetlagged Americans were tired. We had made it to 8:30pm, it was time to go home and sleep.

At a time that felt both too early and too late, I was woken up by my father because our schedule dictated that it was time to start the day. It was 8:30am. I had slept for 10 hours. It was pitch black outside.

Our primary activity of the day was titled “Snorkeling the Continental Divide." We all agreed that sounded very cool and very cold. In hindsight, I think we’d all agree that this assessment was accurate, though one of us struggled with the snorkeling a bit more than the rest. That one, unfortunately, was me.

The water is so clear that snorkelers are encouraged to drink some. In my case, this meant accidentally swallowing a bunch through my nose and coughing in a manner that might suggest that I am quite bad at only breathing through my mouth and at activities (like snorkeling) that require that proficiency.

In between the many breaks I had to take though, I was in awe of the clarity of the water and the sights below. At Crater Lake in Oregon, the saying goes that “you can drop your camera into the water and watch it float away from you for over a minute."

The clarity of this water was on a level even above that, as evidenced by these nice photos that I found on the internet:

The time in the water only totalled about 20 minutes, which was probably not far from the maximum any of us could’ve reasonably lasted in the icy waters. While the drysuits worked well and the view of the rocky fissure from in the water was difficult to turn away from, fingers and toes excel at getting cold.

Exiting the water, you remember that you’re still in Iceland in November. We hurried back to the vans where we changed, drank hot chocolate, and listened to our guide talk about how this was where they filmed “The Wall" in Game of Thrones (I have not been able to confirm this, though many of the scenes in the North were filmed in Iceland).

After we had changed and warmed up, we headed to Geysir - a large geyser that Wikipedia tells me can hurl water up to 230 ft in the air. It was considerably smaller when we saw it, although I have no proof because both times that I attempted to film it, it only erupted seconds after I spent 5 minutes filming nothing and paused to switch my hands.

We took another scenic drive back to the Airbnb, showered, and had a terrific dinner at Bryggjan Brugghús - a brewpub featuring great beer and even better fish and chips made with local cod. After a quick stop for licorice gelato for those that enjoy the taste of poison (and other flavors for those of us who don’t), we made a full attempt to see the Northern Lights.

To see the Northern Lights in Iceland you need a clear sky and a sufficient amount of solar activity. We knew we’d have the former, but the latter was relatively low. We decided to take our chances and head to, ironically, a lighthouse at the edge of Reykjavik where we would hopefully free ourselves from the light pollution of the city. Apparently we were not the only ones who had this idea, as the lighthouse was surrounded by about 40 parked cars and their passengers, all staring longingly up at the Northern night sky.

In that sky rested a large, rectangular shape that looked like a cloud stretched out above us. It’s slight green tint and occasional movements though revealed its true identity as the target of our operation, albeit a slightly underwhelming one. My phone’s best effort to capture what we saw:

(I didn't even realize you could see the Big Dipper in that photo until I started working on this project and looked more closely.)

After 15 minutes of watching the blob in the sky and convincing ourselves that “this counts as seeing it" and was probably the best we were going to get, we headed back to the AirBnb.

Looking out of the house’s north-facing windows, we saw the same shape that we had seen from the lighthouse, lingering there as if to say “you’re not really going to go to sleep yet, right?" As we sat watching, the shape began to evolve - sections of it grouped together into linear streaks in the sky, shifting and dancing in the sky with its green tint glowing stronger. While the aurora never reached anything that you’d see in National Geographic, it definitely left us satisfied. As it faded into the night, we followed suit and headed to bed.

Our final day in Iceland started with us dropping Jasper off at the airport before commencing the day’s activities. The plan: waterfalls.

We decided to keep moving, make our way back to the car, and move on to our final waterfall of the day. I stubbornly refused a dry sock from Henry.

The sun seemed to be beginning its descent. It was 2:15pm.

30 minutes down the road from Seljalandsfoss, we arrived at Skógafoss - the magnificent, colossal falls featured at the top of this page. The falls sit where the Skóga river flows down an expanse of rolling hills before crashing over a sharp cliff that hugs the base of a neighboring mountain. From the top of the falls, we stood in awe of the scene before us.

At this point, we only had about an hour of Icelandic sunlight remaining (our 7:45am departure the next morning would certainly take place in darkness) and we had identified two potential options: a hike to a 1973 US planewreck or exploring the nearby Sólheimajökull glacier. It seemed a little sacrilegious to go to Iceland and not see a glacier, plus I think we were all wary of going to see a wrecked vehicle after growing up around the slightly underwhelming wreck of the Peter Iredale in Oregon. The glacier was an easy choice.

We made the short drive up the road and pulled into a volcanic-gravel parking lot just as a series of buses exited with their large glacier tour groups on board. A few minutes walk down a trail from the parking lot brought us to the glacier. It was striking:

The rest of our time was rather uneventful: a long drive back to Reykjavík, a yummy fish, lamb, and veggie taco dinner, and an early flight the next morning to Copenhagen where Jasper would be playing the role of tour guide. To anyone who has made it this far, thank you for reading! I hope you enjoyed it, and welcome any and all feedback!

For those considering a trip to Iceland, I would strongly encourage you to go. It is a beautiful place - one that I hope to return to and explore more. In hindsight, we regretted our decision to spend every night in Reykjavík. The better approach seems to be to drive around the country, spending nights in different places along the way.

And even though the summer is probably the most popular time to go for a reason, we did love our late November trip under the ever-setting sun.
